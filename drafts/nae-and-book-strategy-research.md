# Bottom line

**The realistic Sept/Oct 2026 trade-book deadline is achievable only as an "agreement-in-principle with an agent submitting" — not as a fully signed Big-Five contract.** The standard pre-thesis-to-signed-contract path runs 7–10 months. To hit Sept/Oct you'd need to compress aggressively via warm intros, a polished proposal in hand before querying, and acceptance that "contract by Oct" probably means "agent signed by July, editor offers in Sept/Oct, signed paper in Nov/Dec."

**Separately and importantly: the NAE Class of 2027 nomination window just closed (Feb–May 2026).** Earliest viable election is Class of 2028, with nominations submitted Feb–May 2027. That gives you roughly nine months to position the site and the public record before that window opens — which is actually a much more comfortable runway than the book deadline.

**Recommended single thesis: Universities-Build-Founders, framed through a contemporary industrial-policy lens.** Pure white space in trade press (no Saxenian successor since 2006), strongest direct NAE fit (Section 12 = Special Fields and Interdisciplinary Engineering, where Doerr, the Tesla founders, Cochran, and the Byers/Seelig Gordon Prize tradition all live), and built on your single longest-running research program (40 years of Stanford alumni data with Bill Miller). The industrial-policy contemporary frame ("why some places build founders and others don't, and what the IRA/China-tech moment tells us about engineering an innovation ecosystem") gives it the news hook trade editors need without putting you head-to-head with Dan Wang's *Breakneck*.

# 1. Ranked thesis recommendation

## Thesis A — Universities-Build-Founders [RECOMMENDED]

**The case for.** Saxenian's *Regional Advantage* (1994) and *New Argonauts* (2006) are the genre-defining books on regional innovation engines — and she's published nothing in the slot since. Margaret O'Mara's *The Code* (Penguin Press, 2019) is now seven years old and isn't a sales hit. There is essentially **no recent trade book that synthesizes 40 years of empirical alumni-impact data** with the methodological rigor your work has. This is white space.

Your existing materials map cleanly: 40 years of Stanford data with Bill Miller, the comparable MIT alumni study, the UVA Darden study, the Lee/Eesley 2018 Research Policy paper on immigrant founders, and the new Founding Team Composition working paper give you the empirical spine. Stanford GSB's own 2025 report documents ~350 new companies/year from GSB alumni alone (https://www.gsb.stanford.edu/newsroom/school-news/quantifying-stanford-gsbs-founder-effect), which is the kind of contemporary statistic editors want.

**The NAE alignment is exceptional.** NAE Section 12 (Special Fields and Interdisciplinary Engineering) is explicitly scoped to include "innovation in education and systems engineering and management" (https://www.nae.edu/166166/Sections). Tom Byers and Tina Seelig won the NAE Bernard M. Gordon Prize in 2009 for engineering-entrepreneurship education at Stanford STVP — your own program (https://engineering.stanford.edu/news/entrepreneurship-program-wins-recognition). John Doerr was elected to NAE Class of 2026 specifically for "leadership in enabling digital transformation through strategic investment, mentorship, and the successful commercialization of innovative technology ventures" (https://www.nae.edu/345149/NAENewClass2026). Tesla co-founders Tarpenning and Eberhard got in for "entrepreneurship in transportation." Cochran (Stanford, Class of 2025) for "biotechnology entrepreneurship." The Academy is actively rewarding ecosystem-builder narratives, and your work is squarely in that lineage.

**The risk.** No recent trade bestseller proves the audience. *The Code* was a quiet seller. You'd need a strong narrative spine to break out of "policy-wonk" shelving — probably anchored on specific founder case studies threaded through the institutional argument.

**The contemporary-frame variant** (recommended). Position the book as *"Why some places build founders, and what the IRA/China-tech moment reveals about engineering innovation ecosystems"* — keeps the alumni-data spine, adds the industrial-policy hook, gives editors something current to pitch.

**The case for.** The most active editorial category in trade press right now. Chris Miller's *Chip War* (Scribner, 2022) won the 2022 FT/Schroders Business Book of the Year (https://www.thebookseller.com/news/miller-wins-30000-financial-times-business-book-of-the-year-award-for-engaging-chip-war) and remains a sustained backlist seller. An editor at any of the houses above will say "we already have our 2025–26 China-industrial-policy book."

**The way through this.** Your empirical-paper credentials (the SMJ paper on export controls and Chinese VC, the IRA-cleantech paper, the China battery subsidies paper) differentiate from Wang's reportorial frame. If you go this direction, lead with the IRA-cleantech / Policy Credibility Index angle — Wang doesn't address that and it's the contemporary US-policy hook editors will care about.

# 2. Timeline — May 28 → October 2026 (revised expectations)

## Reality check from the research

Per Jane Friedman, the canonical industry guide, full trade proposals run 10–25 double-spaced pages excluding sample chapters, 50+ with samples (https://janefriedman.com/start-here-how-to-write-a-book-proposal/). The aggregate sequential pre-thesis-to-signed-contract timeline:

| Phase | Optimistic | Realistic | Pessimistic |
|---|---|---|---|
| Thesis lock + outline | 2 wk | 4–6 wk | 8–12 wk |
| Proposal drafting | 3–4 wk | 6–10 wk | 3+ mo |
| Sample chapter (8–10k words) | ~1 mo | 1–2 mo | 3 mo |
| Query agents → response | 2 wk | 6–8 wk | 3+ mo |
| Agent revisions before submission | 2–4 wk | 1–3 mo | 6+ mo |
| Editor submission → offers | 2 wk | 4–8 wk | 3–6 mo |
| Auction / negotiation | 1–2 wk | 2–4 wk | 6+ wk |
| Contract drafting → signature | 4–6 wk | 2–3 mo | 6 mo |
| **Aggregate (sequential)** | **~4 mo** | **~7–10 mo** | **12–18+ mo** |

## What the agents tell us about compression for someone with your credentials

Per the research (Nieman Storyboard, Jane Friedman, Erik Angner's "Trade Secrets"), a Stanford full prof with a *Nature* paper, 6,800+ citations, and prior Bloomberg coverage typically:
- Gets **warm intros via published colleagues** — bypasses slush, compresses query phase from 8 weeks to ~1 week
- **Is invited to submit a full proposal directly** rather than running a query letter campaign
- Sees **agent responses in 1–3 weeks**, not the standard 6+ weeks
- May go to a **small auction of 3–6 agents** rather than 30-query rounds
- **Realistic agent-signing window with warm intro and ready proposal: 4–8 weeks** (vs. 2–3 months without)

# 7. Risks + mitigations

1. **Thesis lock takes 8+ weeks instead of 4** — the single biggest schedule risk. Pre-thesis is genuinely the hardest phase to compress.
   - **Mitigation:** lock by July 4 via a one-week "thesis sprint" — daily 90-min sessions structured around comp-title analysis, 1-pager drafts, agent-pitch-language testing. If still unlocked by July 11, accept the deadline slip and pick the highest-confidence direction (Thesis A) rather than continuing to oscillate.

2. **Dan Wang's *Breakneck* (Aug 2025) already filled the China-policy slot for 2025–26.** Thesis B is harder than market signals suggest.
   - **Mitigation:** if pursuing B, lead with the IRA-cleantech / Policy Credibility Index angle (Wang doesn't address it); pre-empt the editor's "we have Wang" objection in the proposal's market section.

3. **Pre-thesis + no agent at 4 months out is genuinely tight for a Big-Five trade contract.** The realistic timeline is 7–10 months. Sept/Oct as a signed-paper deadline is the optimistic edge.
   - **Mitigation:** redefine the target as "agent signed by early Aug, editor submission round in Sept, offer in hand by Oct" — with signature in Nov/Dec. Communicate that revised target to any stakeholders who set the original deadline.
   - **Parallel mitigation:** open a university-press track in parallel. Stanford UP (Margo Fleming's old shop), Harvard UP, Princeton UP, MIT Press all accept direct submissions, often without agents, with significantly faster turnaround. A Princeton or Stanford UP contract by October would meet the spirit of the deadline even if Big-Five trade slips to Q4.

4. **Mollick and Hao have made the AI category hard to sell into.** Thesis C is the weakest path.
   - **Mitigation:** if AI must be part of the book, embed it as a chapter inside Thesis A's institutional-engineering frame ("what AI is doing to the institutional environment for founders") rather than the spine.

5. **NAE nomination requires an NAE-member nominator you may not yet have lined up.** Even strong candidacies fail at the nominator-recruitment step.
   - **Mitigation:** start the nominator conversation **this summer**, not next spring. The 60-day target is to identify and informally approach a willing nominator before the website-positioning work matures. Stanford's VPDoR / SoE dean's office is the fastest route to a candid read on who's already supportive.
